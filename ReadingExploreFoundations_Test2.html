Digging for the Past Lesson A • Questions 1–5

[A] When I was in third grade, I watched a video that recreated the discovery of King Tutankhamen's tomb. I remember Howard Carter1 peering through a narrow hole in the tomb with a candle. A workman asked what he could see: "Wonderful things!" Carter said. From that moment, I knew what I wanted to do with my life.

[B] Today, I'm lucky to work as an archeologist. I study the lives of people who lived in Egypt's Nile Delta. It's exciting work. Sometimes you find something that was buried three thousand years ago. Holding a piece of history is an amazing feeling.

[C] So I decided what I wanted to be at the age of nine, and I made it happen. Seems easy, right? Not quite! The road wasn't easy. I did my studies — university, then a PhD. But along the way I was told many things: I was too young, I wasn't qualified, I should get married and have kids. I also needed money, so I applied for six grants.2 The first five replies I got said no. Six months later, the last reply came: This time it was yes. Finally, I could start my own excavation.3

[D] For anyone thinking about a career, I would say: Never give up. If you want to do something, keep trying. If people tell you no, use that — it will push you. At the beginning, it hurts. But the next no hurts a little less. It makes you stronger and actually helps you.

[E] Second, focus on your goals. And I stress yours. You don't have to follow other people. If they say something has never been done, make it happen. You may be the first to do it! There are always challenges, but you overcome them in the end.

1 Howard Carter was a British archeologist who became famous for discovering the tomb of Tutankhamen in 1922.
2 A grant is money given by a government or organization for a special project.
3 An excavation involves removing earth to search for very old objects buried in the ground.

Howard Carter Lesson A • Questions 6–10

Imagine spending your whole life dreaming of making a historic archeological find, and one day that dream comes true. This is what happened for Howard Carter when he discovered King Tutankhamen's tomb in 1922.

British archeologist Howard Carter became interested in "Egyptology" when he was just a boy. Many people in England were fascinated by Egypt's history at that time. As a child, Carter spent a lot of his time studying ancient Egyptian objects, and he was also a talented artist. At 17 years old, his family suggested that he go to Egypt as an archeologist's assistant. His job was to draw pictures of the objects the archeologist found, and to keep a record of them. The work suited him, and he spent many years doing similar work.

Carter's most famous discovery — as is the case with many discoveries — happened by accident. A young Egyptian boy was digging in the sand with a stick, when he found a stone step. From that step, Howard and his team uncovered a set of stairs with a closed door at the bottom of it. When they opened the door, they discovered it was the entrance to King Tutankhamen's tomb.

Carter was asked if he saw anything past the door. He answered, "Yes, wonderful things!" It took around ten years to excavate the tomb. Many items were sent to museums around the world, and, thanks to Carter, everyone got to see a piece of history.

Getting the Shot Lesson B • Questions 24–28

Joel Sartore is a writer, teacher, and photographer. His words — and beautiful images — show his love of photography and of the natural world. He was National Geographic's 2018 Explorer of the Year.

Question 1: How did you become a National Geographic photographer?

[B] My first job was for a newspaper. After a few years there, I met a National Geographic photographer. He liked my photos and said I should send some to the magazine. So I did. That led to a one-day job. And that led to a nine-day job, and so on.

Question 2: What kind of photographers is National Geographic looking for?

[C] To get into National Geographic, you have to give them something they don't have. It's not enough just to be a great photographer. You also have to be a scientist, for example, or be able to dive under sea ice, or spend maybe several days in a tree.

Question 3: Is it difficult to get a job as a photographer today?

[D] It's now more difficult to work for magazines. Technology makes it easy to take good pictures, which means there are more photos and photographers. Also, the Web is full of photos from all around the world that are free, or cost very little. These photos are often good enough to be put in books and magazines that once paid for photographers and their photos.

Question 4: I want to be a photographer. Do you have any advice for me?

[E] Advice? Well, work hard. Be passionate1 about every project you work on. Take lots of pictures in different situations. Look at others' photos thoughtfully and learn from them. And be curious2 about life. There's something to photograph everywhere.

[F] But be a photographer for the right reasons. If you do it for the money, you probably won't really be happy. Do you want to make the world a better place, or make people see things in a different way? If so, you'll enjoy the work much more.

1 A passionate person has very strong feelings about something.
2 If you are curious about something, you want to know more about it.

Guarding a Ghost Town Lesson B • Questions 29–38

[A] As tourists walk through the ghost town of Bodie, California, national park rangers watch them closely. During the tourist season, visitors often pick up objects from the empty buildings, and it's the rangers' job to keep Bodie in good condition.

[B] Many miners1 moved to Bodie during the California Gold Rush. During the 1840s, thousands of people moved westward in search of gold. One such man, William Bodey, discovered gold in the hills not far from present-day Bodie. A mine was built in the area, and the town of Bodie2 was established. The new town attracted thousands of miners, and it grew quickly. Soon, it had a bank, a general store, and two beautiful churches.

[C] But the town did not last. The gold eventually ran out, and miners had nothing left to mine. Businesses started to close, and people moved on, in search of a better life. Bodie became a ghost town.

[D] In winter, snow covers the ground, and the temperatures drop. Even tourists stop coming, and only rangers like Taylor Jackson are left. Jackson is one of five rangers who stay to protect the town. It's not an easy job, but for Jackson and his team, they love it, and the challenge is worth it.

1 Miners are workers who dig for valuable materials, e.g., gold, in the ground.
2 The spelling of the town's name was changed from "Bodey" to "Bodie."
